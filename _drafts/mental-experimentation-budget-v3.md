debugging hooked me.

i started using agentic workflows to dig into production issues and something clicked. i could take raw data, ask questions about it, connect to multiple repos, use the observability provider's cli, and just start moving. data to information to knowledge to insight, fast. faster than i'd ever experienced. i wasn't just debugging, i was making sense of systems in real time.

i built tools, tried new patterns, ran live debugging sessions with my team, recorded them, extracted insights, made highlight videos, iterated on the skills, and started sharing. the momentum was real.

then i was checking in on someone who'd been using these tools. they'd been crushing it. real output, real wins, days of genuine momentum. then the tool didn't find something they knew existed. and their conclusion: "i just can't trust it anymore."

hold up. how? you've been crushing it for days. one miss and that's the takeaway?

my gut said frustration. but i caught myself. this was curiosity. how is it that you can't trust it? what's actually happening here?

as i've been thinking on this, the concept of a mental experimentation budget started taking shape. let's dig into the framework i've been noodling on.

## the mental experimentation budget

what's your capacity to keep trying new things, new workflows, new patterns? how fast is it draining as you experiment? what's the cost of each new attempt?

that's the mental experimentation budget. it's not fixed. it fluctuates. and it has structure.

## the variables

**capacity.** your total budget at any given point. this shifts with sleep, energy, emotional state, time of day, what happened in your last meeting. it's not a personality trait. it's a condition. it changes.

**marginal cost.** what it costs to run one more experiment. when everything is brand new, the cost is high. every interaction takes effort, attention, active decision-making. but as you build fluency, as patterns become automatic, the cost per experiment drops. you stop thinking about how to do the thing and you just do it.

**fixed cost.** the startup cost of beginning at all. the decision to try. the context switch. getting oriented in a new tool or a new way of thinking. this cost is real and it hits before the experiment even starts.

**outcome shock.** the cost that comes from the result. when an experiment goes the way you expected, the cost is manageable. expected outcomes are cheap to process. but when something unexpected happens, when you know something is there and the tool isn't finding it, that's a spike. the cost shoots up. and if your budget is already low when the shock hits, it can be a total drain.

**return.** not every experiment is a cost. some deposit back into the budget. a win gives you confidence, knowledge, momentum. success compounds when you've got the budget to keep going. returns are what make experimentation sustainable, not just tolerable.

**recharge.** the budget replenishes. sleep, movement, flow states, small wins, time away. these are inputs back into the system. the budget isn't a battery that only drains. it's a living resource that fluctuates.

## the model

in cosmic farmland math:

> remaining budget = capacity - fixed cost - (marginal cost × experiments) - outcome shocks + returns + recharge

every term matters, and they all interact.

the person i was checking in on had been building fluency. marginal costs were dropping, returns from wins were compounding. but each experiment was also drawing down their capacity, and they didn't notice. when the outcome shock hit, their budget was already lower than they realized. the shock didn't just cost more than expected. it cost more than they had.

not one failure erasing weeks of success. the failure arriving at a moment when the budget couldn't absorb it.

my experience with the same tools, same kinds of failures, felt completely different. my capacity had headroom. my marginal costs were lower from more time with these patterns. when shocks hit, i could absorb them and keep going. the math was different.

the system has feedback loops too. success lowers future marginal costs (you've built fluency). returns increase capacity (confidence expands what you're willing to try). but shocks can create negative loops. one bad outcome raises the perceived cost of the next experiment, which means you need more budget to try again, which means you're less likely to get the return that would restore it. that's how a single failure becomes a full stop.

## connections

i taught middle school math and coached sports before getting into software. i spent years digging into how people learn, how understanding builds and then sometimes just collapses. humans are sense-making machines, and i got obsessed with the mechanics of it. what conditions let someone integrate new knowledge, and what conditions cause them to reject it?

that obsession led me into cognitive science and psychology, and what i found there maps onto this model.

kahneman's [thinking, fast and slow](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/11468377-thinking-fast-and-slow) describes system 1 and system 2, the fast automatic brain and the slow deliberate one. when you're experimenting and it's going well, system 2 is engaged, learning, processing, building. when an outcome shock hits, system 1 takes over. something faster than rational assessment, operating on pattern and instinct.

raymond prior's [golf beneath the surface](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/61657538-golf-beneath-the-surface) calls it the old brain and the young brain. the young brain is where deliberate thinking happens. the old brain is where perceived threat lives. under stress, the old brain wins. every time. the person on my team wasn't choosing to ignore weeks of wins. their old brain was making that choice for them.

donella meadows, in [thinking in systems](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/3828902-thinking-in-systems), frames the budget as a stock. experiments are outflows. recharge is inflow. the feedback loops (reinforcing and balancing) are where the behavior of the system gets determined. you don't change the system by pushing harder. you find the leverage points.

## putting it into practice

i've been sitting with this model for a while, sharing it, watching it resonate with people wrestling with the same thing. sense-making is one of our greatest capacities. when we have language for something we were already feeling, something shifts.

some questions i keep coming back to:

- what's my budget right now?
- how do i reduce the fixed cost of starting?
- how do i build automaticity and reduce the marginal cost per experiment?
- am i aware of my recharge patterns? am i honest with myself about them?
- how do i increase my overall capacity?
- how do i build resiliency to outcome shocks?
- how do i make recharging more intentional?
- am i pairing the type of experimentation with where i'm at?

this model gives me language for reasoning and sense-making. that feels worth sharing.

does this resonate? what's missing? what would you add?
