## the drowning

open slack. scroll. scroll. scroll.

every message looks the same. the same structure, the same tone, the same helpful bullet points followed by the same friendly sign-off. someone summarized a meeting and it reads like a press release from a company that sells nothing. someone shared an update and it has six emojis i'm pretty sure no human being has ever used with sincerity. the rocket ship. the pointing finger. the sparkles. always the sparkles.

and the walls of text. enormous blocks of perfectly formatted, beautifully structured, absolutely soulless text. paragraphs that say everything and communicate nothing. you can feel the template underneath it. you can feel the optimization. every word chosen not because someone meant it but because the algorithm suggested it was the right word for this context in this channel for this audience.

switch to linkedin. oh god, linkedin.

"i told my team something vulnerable and learned a powerful lesson." "here are 7 things i wish i'd known." "i failed publicly and here's why that's actually a superpower." the same post, rearranged, posted by ten thousand different people who all somehow had the same revelation on the same tuesday. the engagement pods liking each other's stuff. the comments that are just other people's posts in disguise. the whole thing is a machine pretending to be a conversation.

it's everywhere. it's in the emails. it's in the documentation. it's in the blog posts that rank on page one because they were engineered to rank on page one, not because anyone had something to say.

and i'm drowning in it.

## the diagnosis

here's what's actually happening: everything is converging. ai-augmented writing, seo optimization, linkedin engagement formulas, slack summarizers, corporate comms training. all of these forces pushing in the same direction. toward the mean. toward the safe middle. toward the thing that offends no one and moves no one and sounds like it was produced by a committee of pleasant robots.

it's not that any one person is doing something wrong. it's that the systems reward sameness. write something weird and the algorithm buries it. write something raw and the engagement drops. write something that actually sounds like you and the optimization layer suggests you smooth it out, tighten it up, make it more accessible. accessible. what a word. accessible to whom? to everyone, which means to no one. the personality gets optimized right out of it.

and here's the deeper cut, the thing that actually makes my skin crawl: i can't tell if anyone is there anymore.

is that a person? is there a human on the other end of this slack message or did they paste their rough thought into a tool and ship whatever came back? did they read what they sent me? did they engage with what i said or did they engage with a summary of what i said? are we talking to each other or are our agents talking to each other while we do something else?

the person disappeared. not because they left, but because the tools made it so easy to never show up in the first place. you can ship text without writing. you can respond without reading. you can participate in a conversation without being present for any of it.

and what's left is slop. polished, grammatically perfect, absolutely vacant slop.

bland convergence. that's what this is. everything racing to be the most professionally acceptable, algorithmically optimized, engagement-friendly version of nothing. the soul got optimized out and nobody noticed because the formatting was so clean.

## the historical beat

this isn't new. this pattern has a history.

hunter s. thompson didn't write gonzo because he wanted to be weird. he wrote it because the journalism of his era had become so sanitized, so careful, so committed to the illusion of objectivity that it couldn't capture what was actually happening. the conventions were lies. the "balanced" reporting was a kind of cowardice. so he blew it up. fear and loathing in las vegas. on the campaign trail. the kentucky derby piece that remains, decades later, one of the most alive things ever written about a sporting event.

i found thompson in college. espn page 2 columns at first, the surface-level stuff. then fear and loathing and the doors came off. this is possible? you can do this? and then the letters, the campaign reporting, the full body of work. a person who wrote like his life depended on it because he understood that when the pressure to conform gets high enough, the only honest response is to refuse. not to be provocative for its own sake. to be real when real has become the radical act.

the conditions create the response. when conformity is enforced, whether by algorithms or authoritarian impulse or the gentle pressure of professional norms, authentic creative expression doesn't just become more valuable. it becomes necessary.

## the declaration

so here's where i am.

i'm a creative person. i've always been one, even through the years where i convinced myself otherwise, where the job title didn't include the word "creative" so i figured that part of me was a hobby at best. it wasn't. it was the thread running underneath everything.

and right now, in this moment, when everything is converging toward the blandest possible version of human expression, i'm choosing to go the other way.

not provocative for shock value. not weird for the sake of weird. one degree off normal. just enough to make you go "wait, that's a person." just enough that you can feel someone is actually here, actually writing this, actually meaning it. irreverence with substance. the kind of creative expression that makes people slightly uncomfortable because it's real, and real has become uncommon enough to be startling.

this is my response to the current moment. not a critique from the sidelines. not a think piece about how ai is ruining writing. this post, right here, the way it sounds, the way it moves, the way it refuses to be optimized for your engagement metrics. this is the thing itself.

authenticity is where the deep connection lives. it's where you find the people who are actually there. who actually read what you wrote and responded to what you said, not to a summary of what you said. who write like themselves even when the tools make it so easy not to.

i don't know exactly where this goes. i know that getting weird feels freeing in a way i haven't felt in a long time. i know that writing like this, in my actual voice, with my actual brain doing the actual work, feels like resistance in the best possible sense. i know that the bland convergence is real and i know that the response to it is not to write a thoughtful essay about the importance of voice. the response is to use yours.

so use yours. please. i'm begging you. be alive in your writing. be a person. be the one degree off normal that makes someone stop scrolling and think "oh, there's someone actually there."

the world has enough sparkle emojis. it does not have enough you.
