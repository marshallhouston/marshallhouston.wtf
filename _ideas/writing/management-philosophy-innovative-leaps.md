## origin

conversation with someone exploring becoming an eng manager. he wanted to know how my team had landed significant step-change improvements across the org while also balancing standard product roadmap work.

he framed it as two lenses:
- how do we find the time?
- how do we generate the ideas?

my answer: primarily two factors.
1. high context to enable decision velocity.
2. baking slack into the system.

## factor 1: high context

i want to be extremely high context with the business.
- where is it going
- why is it going there
- how do we fit into it
- what problems keep our eng and product leaders up at night
- what is coming down the business development pipeline

i pitch ideas there. listen to the feedback, the narrative, the pushback, the opportunity. adjust my narrative based on it.

- where will we face pushback?
- where will this resonate?
- who are our champions?
- who will be the biggest skeptics?

then i provide that context directly to engineers. we talk about it *all the time*. it is so critical.

i want engineers to be product-minded. entrepreneurial. once they have the context, they should own the decisions.

- no capital A Alignment.
- no capital P Process.
- you own decisions. build your framework. what is a reasonable choice. make the decision. move on.

i do not want to be a blocker. if they come to me with a decision that needs to be made, does it actually require me, or can i push it back down to them to own it?

i trust them to make good decisions and surface them to me if they're blocked or above their decision making authority. then i take that extremely seriously, and we make that decision as quickly as reasonably possible.

do not weaponize hindsight. trust you, make the best decision now, and then go for it.

high agency, high context, get me out of the way.

## factor 2: slack baked in

will larson. management tactics across different phases of teams. innovating, add slack. repaying debt, add time.

- can't be a science project. must have business connection.
- asymmetric upside. non-linear bets.
- plant lots of seeds.
- intellectual honesty. quit criteria. gates to proceed.

this is about deliberately, strategically, structurally providing space for r&d, growth, development, planting seeds.

reference: https://lethain.com/durably-excellent-teams/

## the transparency caveat

adding slack into the system requires two things:

a) we continue to deliver impact so that stakeholders and the business are getting what they need.
b) i'm transparent with the team about what's being required.

there will be times we have to swarm and focus on a high-priority business piece and spend less time on innovation.

it cannot be a lever i pull all the time. and when it has to be pulled, it must be:
- direct
- transparent
- full of context and the "why"

if i see the winds shifting, i want to give people a heads up so they can make reasonable tradeoffs. get that v0, v1, next version in before we have to pivot. not always possible to give the heads up, but where possible, i want the team to understand the overall context and give them time and focus to hit a stopping point until we can pick back up again.
