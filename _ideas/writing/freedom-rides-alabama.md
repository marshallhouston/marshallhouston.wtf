Writings and tweets from the Freedom Rides. The power of human possibility. Reflections on being an Alabamian.

Wow.

## sources found (2026-03-31)

- **GPB/PBS video profile**: https://www.gpb.org/television/show/american-experience/season/23/freedom-riders/extras/marshall-houston-student
  - one of 40 students selected nationwide from ~1,000 applicants
  - Day 2 video: posed the questions "What is my identity? What are my beliefs? And what are my actions?"
  - Day 6: produced a video of fellow rider Davy Knittle rapping about nonviolence. caption: "Nonviolence is creative."

- **NBC News article** (May 7, 2011): https://www.nbcnews.com/id/wbna42943337
  - direct quotes from 22-year-old Marshall:
  - "Through that process, I really first began to understand what power strategic actions and a coalition of students who believe in equality and justice can have in society."
  - "It's inspiring when you see young people my age taking a stand. If I were in that situation, would I take that stand? That's not something you can answer until that moment comes."

- **University of Alabama news release** (April 7, 2011): https://archive.news.ua.edu/2011/04/ua-student-selected-to-board-the-bus-for-freedom-ride-commemoration/
  - selected based on essay, civic engagement through social media, extracurricular involvement
  - had received the Algernon Sydney Sullivan Award
  - created documentary films on Foster Auditorium's integration history (Wallace's "stand in the schoolhouse door") and border violence in El Paso
  - student chair of UA's Blackburn Institute
  - 10-day journey May 6-16, 2011: Atlanta, Anniston, Montgomery, New Orleans

## notes (2026-03-31)

almost 15 years ago. the questions from Day 2 are still the questions. "what is my identity? what are my beliefs? what are my actions?" that's not a coincidence, that's the throughline.

the compression is wild in hindsight: made a documentary about UA's integration history -> selected for the Freedom Ride -> graduated -> moved across the country -> taught. no pause. 22 years old.

and the Foster Auditorium documentary first: you were already digging into this history before the ride. the ride didn't create the interest, it deepened something already there.

the quote about "would I take that stand?" is interesting from this distance. you did take a stand, repeatedly, in different forms. the question was almost rhetorical in the moment but it turned out to be a preview.
