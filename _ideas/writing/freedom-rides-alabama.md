## notes (2026-03-31)

almost 15 years ago. the questions from day 2 are still the questions. "what is my identity? what are my beliefs? what are my actions?" that's not a coincidence, that's the throughline.

the compression is wild in hindsight: made a documentary about ua's integration history -> selected for the freedom ride -> graduated -> moved across the country -> taught. no pause. 22 years old.

and the foster auditorium documentary first: you were already digging into this history before the ride. the ride didn't create the interest, it deepened something already there.

the quote about "would i take that stand?" is interesting from this distance. you did take a stand, repeatedly, in different forms. the question was almost rhetorical in the moment but it turned out to be a preview.

## from the blog and archives (2026-03-31)

reference files in vault: `freedom-ride-content-recovery.md` and `bama-on-the-bus-complete-backup.md`

### the identity line

from "reminders of tuscaloosa" (may 18, 2011):
> "the trip was an exercise in personal identity. for years, i had tried unsuccessfully to reconcile my family's history -- at once i'm a 7th generation alabamian with plantation owning ancestors, a great, great-grandson of a cherokee, and a great-grandson of a poor sharecropper who had 13 children. throughout the ride, i came to understand my family history in new terms -- terms without shame or guilt -- but also terms that will never settle for oppressive or unjust systems in society."

this is the line. 22 years old, trying to hold all of that at once. plantation owners and sharecroppers and cherokee in the same bloodline. and the ride gave him a frame for it.

### the tornado post

"walking in tuscaloosa" (may 9, 2011) is the most raw thing in the archive. can't sleep, tuscaloosa on his mind while retracing the freedom riders' route. the internal conflict: documentarian vs. able-bodied young man who should be helping. his father's example, rising at dawn and returning at dusk covered in grime after tornado damage when marshall was a boy. "pops had already made that decision for me with his actions over 15 years prior." chose to do the work instead of filming it. "i played my role, and i played it well."

the tornado was april 27. the freedom ride started may 6. nine days.

### the compression, in sequence

- april 27, 2011: ef4 tornado destroys tuscaloosa. chainsaw days and firepit nights at the ato house.
- may 3: leaves tuscaloosa for the last time as an undergraduate
- may 6-16: 2011 student freedom ride. dc to new orleans. 1,500 miles.
- may 17: already in nashville, writing open letters to the tuscaloosa mayor about sustainable redevelopment
- may 19: mails the letter
- may 20: interview on the michael eric dyson radio show
- june 7: arrives in phoenix for teach for america institute
- summer 2011: teaching 3rd/4th grade math at valley view
- fall 2011: teaching 7th grade math at west denver prep in denver

22 years old. no pause.

### the application essay

posted his full application responses on may 6. key passage:
> "before i embarked on this journey, my knowledge of foster auditorium only consisted of short clips from movies like forrest gump... over the course of the year, i began to see foster auditorium as more than a building -- it now represented the courageous actions of the students who peacefully integrated the university of alabama. one week before our film screened in front of an audience of over one thousand people, university administrators announced that the building would be renovated."

the documentary preceded the ride. the ride didn't create the interest. it deepened something that was already there.

### diane nash and agapic energy

day 1 notes from diane nash's lecture. she reframed nonviolence as agapic energy, from the greek "agape." not pacifism, activism. "we have something to fight with too; it is an energy generated by love!"

first principle: "people are never your enemy! the enemies are the unjust political systems, unjust economic systems, and attitudes."

second principle: "oppression always requires the cooperation of the oppressed."

### joan mulholland

from the pbs dispatches (day 4, may 11): met joan mulholland at the international civil rights museum. discovered shared huguenot heritage through charleston. she declared them "soulmates" and "family."

### uncle val mcgee

from "inspirations" (may 11): great uncle val mcgee. retired trial judge in dale county, al. went to law school with harper lee before she dropped out to become a writer. also there with hugo black. wrote a book called "selma" at age 88. cousin larry (married to william faulkner's niece dean) helped publish it. "they had very different perspectives politically but loved each other dearly."

the literary connections in this family are absurd.

### the q&a series

created a 5-part civic engagement q&a series during the ride. interviewed:
- ellen east (evp, time warner cable, ua grad)
- lecia brooks (splc outreach director)
- andy grace (documentary filmmaker, ua professor, the one who encouraged him to apply)
- matt wilson (entrepreneur, gubernatorial debate collaborator)
- jimmy young (debate coordinator)

22 years old and creating structured interview content while on a 10-day bus ride.

### the poster series

collaborated with jake andrews on a 5-poster series. each poster: one word (courage, equality, justice, determination, truth), each showing a portion of the freedom riders' routes. when placed side by side, they complete the full map. background is a tire texture. emailed creative brief on day 1 (may 8), jake sent back finished designs at 3:32 am on may 10. forwarded to pbs/wgbh crew on may 11. still live on the blog.

### the tadpoles

from the michael eric dyson show post (may 20): crossing the edmund pettus bridge in selma. after the solemn march, saw michellay cole and charles reed laughing at tadpoles in the memorial pool. they'd never seen tadpoles before. city people. marshall grew up with them everywhere. "this realization makes much more appreciative of the uniqueness of the southern thing."

small, perfect moment.

### the bus of life

"a new direction" (may 17): decided to keep the blog going after the ride. "though i'm no longer on the pbs bus, my experiences during the 11-day ride have changed my outlook on life and the way in which i interact with others, so i like to think that i'm still on the bus -- though only in spirit now. 40 deep we roll!"

### his classroom vision (june 7)

> "i will have a classroom community of 'every second learners' where each person learns through discovery, embraces curiosity, and asks questions. we will focus on academic achievement in an atmosphere of equality and respect where vulnerability is celebrated."

the freedom ride language is all over this. the ride became the teaching.

## marshall's reflections (2026-03-31)

this isn't just about the freedom rides. it's about always having been creative. coming from a family of writing and creatives. harper lee adjacent. faulkner adjacent. uncle val writing "selma" at 88. the q&a series at 22. the documentary before that. creativity isn't something that started, it's something that was always there. shaped by family, by alabama, by proximity to storytelling as a way of life.

nostalgia is a thread here too. reading 22-year-old marshall and feeling the distance.

and then the cherokee story. told growing up, part of the family narrative, part of how he understood himself on the ride ("a great, great-grandson of a cherokee"). learned since that some of these stories are false. so now there's a layer on top of that identity passage: the story you told about yourself was itself a story someone told you. memory as inheritance, some of it true, some of it not. what do you do with the parts that aren't?

this connects to the tattoos (circle and two lines meeting at point of tangency). the geometry of identity, things touching at a single point. the person who was and the person who is, meeting but not overlapping.
