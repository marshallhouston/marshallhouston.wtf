title: your experimentation budget — capacity, cost, and cycles
captured: 2026-03-23

a framework for thinking about experimentation as a finite resource to manage.

## core idea

every experiment has a cost. opportunity cost, cognitive cost, marginal cost. everyone has a budget. same experiment, vastly different drawdown depending on your capacity and cost. how to increase your overall capacity, how to decrease the marginal cost of each experiment, and how to know where you are on your budget right now.

## why this matters

the pace of change demands constant experimentation. new tools, new patterns, new ways of working every week. but "just experiment more" without a sustainability model leads to burnout. we need a framework for experimentation that's as rigorous as our frameworks for delivery.

## analogies

draws on endurance sports, formula one, and olympic periodization. you don't go hard all the time; you build cycles.

## fractal nature

the framework applies at the individual, team, and organizational level.

## audience takeaway

a practical lens for sustainable experimentation in a world that demands constant learning.

## fleshed out design (2026-03-25)

### post arc

#### 1. the hook
anecdote: someone experimenting with AI tools, success after success, flying. then one bad outcome and total collapse. "i can't trust any of this." the reaction: wtf? you just had all this success. how does one failure erase all of that? that gap between their reaction and yours is the entry point. something is different. what?

#### 2. the health bar
introduce the mental experimentation budget. think of it like a video game health bar. finite mental resource. every experiment draws from it. some draws are small, some are big. sometimes a hit looks minor but lands when you're already low and it's game over. that person wasn't weak. they just didn't have the budget left to absorb the shock.

#### 3. the model
personal background: econ and english in college. never fit in either room. the power was in the combination, applying economic systems thinking to things that aren't traditionally economic. that's what we're doing here.

the components:
- **total budget (capacity):** overall mental resource for experimentation. dynamic. shifts with sleep, energy, day of the week, emotional state, what just happened.
- **marginal cost per experiment:** what it costs to run one more. high when brand new, low when you've built fluency and automaticity.
- **startup cost (fixed cost):** baseline cost just to begin. deciding to try, context switching, getting oriented.
- **outcome shock (variable cost):** cost from the result. expected outcome? manageable. unexpected? spike. the anecdote person had low marginal costs during success but got hit with a massive outcome shock they couldn't absorb.
- **return on experiment:** not every experiment is a cost. some deposit back. confidence, knowledge, momentum. success compounds when you've got the budget to keep going.
- **recharge:** the budget isn't just depleting, it's fluctuating. sleep, movement, wins, flow states are inputs back in. this is a system, not a battery.

systems thinking lens (Donella Meadows): the mental budget is a stock. experiments are outflows. recharge is inflow. feedback loops and leverage points are where you intervene to change the system behavior.

#### 4. the science
why the model maps to reality. Kahneman's Thinking Fast and Slow (system 1 / system 2). Raymond Pryor's Golf Beneath the Surface (old brain / new brain). cognitive load, threat response, dopamine and reward loops. the one-failure collapse is the old brain hijacking, threat response wiping out rational assessment.

#### 5. now what (exploratory)
the levers exist. you can see them in the model. but exploring, not prescribing:
- can i increase my overall budget? what does that look like?
- can i notice where i am right now? time of day, energy, context?
- can i decrease the startup cost? repetition? familiarity? framing?
- when i get an unexpected outcome, what determines whether it's manageable or total drain? can i build resilience to outcome shocks?
- what are my recharge patterns? do i know them?

tone: i think this model is useful. i'm applying it. i don't have all the answers but i have better questions now.

#### 6. closing
short. natural. don't force it. maybe ties back to the anecdote, maybe a one-liner, maybe just an invitation.
