are you kidding me?

i'd been working with someone on my team, getting them into these ai tools. showed them my flows, walked through how i use them, said go try it. and they did. they were building things, getting wins, moving faster than before. real momentum.

then one bad interaction. one time the tool hallucinated, gave them something wrong, cost them an hour of debugging.

their takeaway? "i can't trust any of this."

are you kidding me? you just had weeks of positive reinforcement. real output, real velocity. and one bad experience erases all of that? how?

my gut reaction was frustration. and then i caught myself.

## wait, what's going on?

because the reaction wasn't irrational. something real was happening. something i wasn't seeing.

i've spent time as an educator and a coach before getting into tech. i've watched people learn. i've watched regressions happen where someone who was making progress suddenly falls back. this felt like that. but i didn't have the right words for why.

so i started pulling at the thread. what's different between my brain and theirs in that moment? why do i absorb a bad outcome and keep going while someone else hits the same wall and stops?

i feel unleashed right now with these tools. genuinely. when something goes wrong, i shrug and try again. but that person, with the same evidence, felt threatened. why?

## what's different about my brain?

i studied economics and english in college. never really fit in either room. the econ folks thought i was too abstract. the english folks thought i was too analytical. but the power was always in the combination, taking economic systems thinking and applying it to things that aren't traditionally economic.

that's what started happening here.

i realized there's a budget. a mental experimentation budget. finite at any given moment, like a health bar in a video game. you know the one. stardew valley, you're working the farm all day, your energy bar is draining. zelda, you're climbing a cliff and watching your stamina shrink. you can regenerate it (eat something, rest, find a shrine) but right now, in this moment, it's finite.

everyone has one. and the size of yours, right now, determines what you can absorb.

okay so there's a budget. what else?

there's a cost per experiment. when everything is brand new, the cost is high. every interaction takes effort, attention, decision-making. but as you build fluency, as things become automatic, the cost per experiment drops. you're not thinking about how to prompt anymore, you're just working.

and there's a startup cost just to begin. the decision to try. the context switch. getting oriented in a new tool, a new flow, a new way of thinking. that cost is real even before the experiment starts.

then there's what i started calling outcome shock. when an experiment goes the way you expected, the cost is manageable. expected outcomes are cheap. but when something unexpected happens, when the tool hallucinates or your code breaks in a way you didn't anticipate, that's a spike. the cost shoots up. and if your budget is already low when the shock hits? game over. you don't have the capacity to absorb it.

that's what happened to that engineer. their marginal costs were low during the success streak (they were building fluency) but they got hit with an outcome shock they couldn't absorb. not because they were weak. because their budget was already drawn down and they didn't know it.

but here's the thing that makes this a system and not just a battery draining. experiments aren't only costs. some deposit back. a win gives you confidence, knowledge, momentum. success compounds when you've got the budget to keep going. and the budget itself recharges. sleep, movement, flow states, small wins. these are inputs back into the system.

donella meadows would call the budget a stock. experiments are outflows. recharge is inflow. and somewhere in the feedback loops and leverage points is where you actually intervene to change the behavior of the whole system.

## oh, that's why

the model clicked for me when i started mapping it to the science.

kahneman talks about system 1 and system 2. the fast, automatic brain and the slow, deliberate brain. when you're experimenting and things are going well, system 2 is engaged, you're learning, processing, building. but when an outcome shock hits, system 1 takes over. threat response. fight or flight. the rational assessment gets overridden by something older and faster.

raymond pryor calls it the old brain and the new brain in his work on golf psychology. the new brain is where your technique lives, your practice, your skill. the old brain is where fear lives. and under stress, the old brain wins. every time.

that one-failure collapse isn't a character flaw. it's the old brain hijacking the controls. threat response wiping out weeks of rational evidence. the person isn't choosing to ignore their positive experiences. their nervous system is doing it for them.

knowing that changes everything about how i think about this. it's not about convincing someone to be more resilient. it's about understanding the system they're operating in.

## better questions

i don't have this figured out. i'm applying it, i'm thinking about it, but i'm exploring, not prescribing.

what i do have is better questions.

can i notice where i am on my budget right now? not in the abstract, but today. this morning. after that last meeting, before this next experiment. am i topped up or running low?

can i decrease the startup cost? does repetition help? familiarity? framing the experiment differently?

when an unexpected outcome hits, what determines whether it's a manageable draw or a total drain? can i build resilience to outcome shocks, or is the move to just not experiment when my budget is low?

what are my recharge patterns? do i actually know them? or am i guessing?

and the big one: can i increase my overall budget? what does that even look like? is it practice? confidence? belief in the possibility?

i think it might be that last one. i believe in the possibility of these tools. deeply. and that belief, that sense of being unleashed, might be the biggest single input into my experimentation budget. not skill, not knowledge. belief.

i'm still digging. but i've got a model now and the model gives me language for something i was feeling but couldn't name. that feels worth sharing, even half-formed. especially half-formed.

here's how i'm thinking about it. i want to see how you're thinking about it.
