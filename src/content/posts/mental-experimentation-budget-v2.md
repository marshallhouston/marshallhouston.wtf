debugging hooked me.

i started using agentic workflows to dig into production issues and something clicked. i could take raw data, ask questions about it, connect to multiple repos, use the observability provider's cli, and just start moving. data to information to knowledge to insight, fast. faster than i'd ever experienced. i wasn't just debugging, i was making sense of systems in real time.

so i ran with it. built tools, tried new patterns, started live debugging sessions and sharing them with my team. "y'all, check this out." and it spread. other people picked it up, started using it, started giving me feedback. the momentum was real.

then something interesting happened.

someone who had been loving it, real success, real velocity, hit a point where the tool didn't find something they expected it to find. and their conclusion was: "i just can't trust it anymore."

hold up. how? you've been crushing it for days. real output, real wins. one miss and that's the takeaway?

i wanted to understand it. how does someone go from momentum to full stop on a single data point? and why didn't the same thing happen to me?

that question led me somewhere.

## the mental experimentation budget

there's a finite resource at play when you're trying new things. a pool of mental capacity available to you at any given moment. it fluctuates, but right now, today, you have some amount of it. like a health bar in a video game. stardew valley, you're working the farm, your energy bar is draining. zelda, you're climbing and watching stamina shrink. you can restore it, but in this moment, it's what you've got.

i'm calling it your mental experimentation budget. here's how i think it works.

## the components

**capacity.** your total budget at any given point. this shifts with sleep, energy, emotional state, time of day, what happened in your last meeting. it's not a personality trait. it's a condition. it changes.

**marginal cost.** what it costs to run one more experiment. when everything is brand new, the cost is high. every interaction takes effort, attention, active decision-making. but as you build fluency, as patterns become automatic, the cost per experiment drops. you stop thinking about how to do the thing and you just do it.

**fixed cost.** the startup cost of beginning at all. the decision to try. the context switch. getting oriented in a new tool or a new way of thinking. this cost is real and it hits before the experiment even starts.

**outcome shock.** the cost that comes from the result. when an experiment goes the way you expected, the cost is manageable. expected outcomes are cheap to process. but when something unexpected happens (the tool hallucinates, your code breaks in a way you didn't anticipate, you don't find what you were sure you'd find) that's a spike. the cost shoots up. and if your budget is already low when the shock hits, it can be a total drain.

**return.** not every experiment is a cost. some deposit back into the budget. a win gives you confidence, knowledge, momentum. success compounds when you've got the budget to keep going. returns are what make experimentation sustainable, not just tolerable.

**recharge.** the budget replenishes. sleep, movement, flow states, small wins, time away. these are inputs back into the system. the budget isn't a battery that only drains. it's a living resource that fluctuates.

## how these pieces fit together

a rough way to think about it:

> remaining budget = capacity - fixed cost - (marginal cost × experiments) - outcome shocks + returns + recharge

this isn't a real equation. don't plug numbers into it. but it's a way of seeing how the pieces push and pull on each other. every term matters, and they all interact.

this is a system, not a checklist.

the person on my team had been building fluency. their marginal costs were dropping, they were getting returns from wins, everything was compounding. but each experiment was also drawing down their capacity, and they didn't notice. when the outcome shock hit (the tool not finding what they expected) their budget was already lower than they realized. the shock didn't just cost more than expected. it cost more than they had.

that's the mechanism. it's not that one failure erased weeks of success. it's that the failure arrived at a moment when the budget couldn't absorb it.

contrast that with my experience. same tools, same kinds of failures along the way. but my capacity was different. i'd been experimenting with these patterns longer, so my marginal costs were lower. i'd built up enough returns that my budget had headroom. when shocks hit, i could absorb them and keep going. not because i'm tougher, but because the math was different.

the system has feedback loops too. success lowers future marginal costs (you've built fluency). returns increase capacity (confidence expands what you're willing to try). but shocks can create negative loops. one bad outcome raises the perceived cost of the next experiment, which means you need more budget to try again, which means you're less likely to get the return that would restore it. that's how a single failure becomes a full stop.

## why i think this model holds up

i taught middle school math and coached sports before getting into software. i spent years digging into how people learn, how they make sense of new information, how understanding builds and then sometimes just collapses. humans are sense-making machines, and i got obsessed with the mechanics of it. what conditions let someone integrate new knowledge, and what conditions cause them to reject it?

that obsession led me into cognitive science and psychology, and what i found there maps directly onto this model.

kahneman talks about system 1 and system 2, the fast automatic brain and the slow deliberate one. when you're experimenting and it's going well, system 2 is engaged. you're learning, processing, building. but when an outcome shock hits, system 1 takes over. threat response. something older and faster than rational assessment.

raymond pryor calls it the old brain and the new brain in his work on golf psychology. the new brain is where your technique lives. the old brain is where fear lives. under stress, the old brain wins. every time.

that one-failure collapse isn't a character flaw. it's the old brain hijacking the controls. threat response overriding weeks of evidence. the person isn't choosing to ignore their wins. their nervous system is making that choice for them.

donella meadows would frame the budget as a stock in a system. experiments are outflows. recharge is inflow. and the feedback loops (both reinforcing and balancing) are where the real behavior of the system gets determined. you don't change the system by pushing harder. you change it by finding the leverage points.

## implications

i don't have this figured out. i'm applying the model, thinking with it, but exploring, not prescribing.

i think there's something here though. and the place it's leading me is toward better questions.

can i notice where i am on my budget right now? not in the abstract. today. this morning. after that last meeting, before this next experiment.

can i decrease the fixed cost of starting? does repetition help? does framing the experiment as small and contained make it cheaper to begin?

when an outcome shock hits, what determines whether it's a manageable draw or a total drain? is there a way to build resilience to shocks, or is the move to just not experiment when the budget is low?

what are my recharge patterns? do i actually know them, or am i guessing?

and the one i keep coming back to: can i increase my overall capacity? what does that even look like? i think it might be belief. i believe in the possibility of these tools. deeply. and that belief might be the single biggest input into the budget. not skill, not knowledge. the sense that this is worth trying.

i'm still working through it. but i've got a model now, and the model gives me language for something i was feeling but couldn't name. that feels worth sharing, even incomplete.

here's how i'm thinking about it. i want to see how you're thinking about it.
